Selling a House During Divorce in Texas – Everything You Need to Know

Published April 27, 2026 · Dallas-Fort Worth, Texas
← All Articles

Going through a divorce is emotionally exhausting, and deciding what to do with your shared home adds another layer of stress to an already difficult situation. If you're facing divorce in Texas and need to sell your house, understanding your options and the legal landscape can help you move forward with confidence and clarity.

Selling a house during divorce in Texas comes with unique challenges that set it apart from a typical home sale. Between community property laws, court timelines, and the emotional weight of letting go of your family home, you're dealing with a lot. This guide will walk you through everything you need to know to make informed decisions during this transition.

Understanding Texas Community Property Laws

Texas is one of nine community property states, which significantly impacts how your home is handled during divorce. Generally speaking, any property acquired during your marriage is considered community property and belongs equally to both spouses—regardless of whose name is on the title.

There are exceptions, of course. Property you owned before marriage, inheritances, and gifts specifically given to one spouse are typically considered separate property. However, things can get complicated if separate property has been commingled with marital assets or if community funds were used for mortgage payments or improvements.

What This Means for Your Home Sale

Because Texas law presumes equal ownership, both spouses typically need to agree on selling the house. Even if only one name is on the deed, if the home was purchased during the marriage with marital funds, it's likely community property. This means:

  • Both spouses usually must consent to the sale
  • Proceeds from the sale are generally split equally (unless you have a different agreement)
  • You'll both need to sign closing documents
  • The court may need to approve the sale depending on your divorce timeline

Your Options When Dividing the Marital Home

When it comes to your house, you typically have three main options during a Texas divorce:

Option 1: Sell the House and Split the Proceeds

This is often the cleanest option, especially when neither spouse can afford the home alone or when you both want a fresh start. Selling allows you to divide the equity and move on without ongoing financial ties to your ex-spouse.

Option 2: One Spouse Buys Out the Other

If one person wants to keep the family home—perhaps to maintain stability for children—they can buy out the other spouse's share. This requires refinancing the mortgage in one name and having enough funds or assets to compensate the other spouse for their equity.

Option 3: Co-Own Temporarily

Some divorcing couples choose to delay selling, often to let children finish school or to wait for better market conditions. This arrangement requires continued cooperation and clear agreements about mortgage payments, maintenance, and eventual sale terms.

For most people going through divorce, selling the house outright offers the most straightforward path to financial independence and emotional closure.

The Timeline: When Can You Sell?

One of the most common questions is: "Can we sell the house before the divorce is final?" The answer depends on your specific situation and how cooperative both parties are.

Selling Before the Divorce is Final

You can sell your house before your divorce is finalized in Texas, but you'll need agreement from both spouses. Many couples prefer this option because it:

  • Eliminates ongoing mortgage payments and maintenance costs
  • Provides funds that can be used for separate living arrangements
  • Removes a major source of potential conflict
  • Simplifies the final divorce decree

However, if there are temporary restraining orders in place (which are standard in Texas divorces), you'll need court approval or written agreement from both parties before listing the property.

Selling After the Divorce Decree

Your divorce decree will outline exactly what happens to the house. It might order the home to be sold with specific terms about listing price, realtor selection, and how proceeds are divided. Following the decree's instructions is crucial to avoid legal complications.

Tax Considerations for Divorce Home Sales in Texas

While Texas doesn't have state income tax, you'll still need to consider federal tax implications when selling your marital home during divorce.

The good news: You may qualify for the capital gains exclusion, which allows you to exclude up to $250,000 in gains if filing individually, or $500,000 if filing jointly. To qualify, you must have owned and lived in the home for at least two of the five years before the sale.

Timing matters here. If you can complete the sale before your divorce is final and file jointly, you might benefit from the larger exclusion. However, this requires cooperation and shouldn't delay your divorce unnecessarily. Consult with a tax professional to understand your specific situation.

Challenges of Traditional Home Sales During Divorce

Selling a house the traditional way is stressful enough without adding divorce to the equation. Many DFW couples face these additional challenges:

Cooperation Requirements

Traditional sales require ongoing cooperation on decisions about pricing, repairs, showings, and offers. When communication has broken down, this can be extremely difficult and prolong the process.

Preparing the House for Market

Getting a house ready to list typically requires repairs, deep cleaning, staging, and constant maintenance for showings. When one or both spouses have already moved out, coordinating this work becomes complicated.

Financial Strain

Continuing to pay the mortgage, utilities, and maintenance on a marital home while also paying for separate living arrangements creates financial pressure that many divorcing couples can't sustain long-term.

Emotional Toll

Keeping the house show-ready, attending showings, and negotiating with buyers extends the emotional difficulty of divorce. Many people just want to move forward with their lives.

Steps to Selling Your House During Divorce in DFW

If you've decided to sell, here's a practical roadmap for moving forward:

1. Communicate and Agree

Both spouses need to agree on selling. If you're working with attorneys or a mediator, make sure this decision is clearly documented.

2. Determine Your Home's Value

Get a realistic assessment of what your home is worth in current DFW market conditions. Consider getting multiple opinions or a professional appraisal.

3. Calculate Your Equity

Determine what you'll actually walk away with after paying off the mortgage, any liens, and selling costs. This helps both parties understand what they're dividing.

4. Decide How to Sell

Choose between a traditional sale with a realtor or a direct sale to a cash buyer. Consider your timeline, the home's condition, and your ability to cooperate through the selling process.

5. Review Court Orders

Make sure you're complying with any temporary orders or divorce decree requirements regarding the sale.

6. Keep Everything Documented

Maintain records of all agreements, expenses, and communications regarding the sale. This protects both parties and ensures the proceeds are divided as agreed.

7. Plan for Proceeds Distribution

Decide in advance how sale proceeds will be handled—typically through your attorneys' trust accounts or as specified in your divorce agreement.
